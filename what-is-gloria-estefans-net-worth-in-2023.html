The Miami Sound Machine and the beginnings of an icon

Before she was Gloria Estefan, a global superstar, she was Gloria María Milagrosa Fajardo García. Born in Havana, Cuba, Gloria’s family fled the Cuban Revolution when she was just a toddler. Little did the world know that this Cuban immigrant would soon revolutionize the music scene.

In 1975, a young Gloria met Emilio Estefan, a charismatic and budding musician. They teamed up musically and romantically by some twist of fate—or perhaps the rhythmic gods winking down at us. They became the heartbeat of the Miami Sound Machine. Together, they churned out hits that made even the most rhythmically challenged feel they had some Latin flair in their step.

Hits like “Conga,” “Bad Boy,” and “Anything For You” quickly propelled Gloria and her squad to international fame. But this wasn’t just another flash-in-the-pan ’80s act. Oh no, Gloria was here to stay. Throughout the ’90s and into the 21st century, Estefan continued producing hits, seamlessly transitioning from pop to ballads and venturing into children’s music. She’s like the Swiss Army knife of the music world—seemingly no end to her talents.

And while her music alone is enough to cement her legacy, Gloria is more than just her hits. She’s a beacon for the immigrant dream, a testament to the idea that talent knows no borders and passion is the universal language. Alongside her husband Emilio, she’s also been a driving force behind the careers of other Latinx artists, proving that her rhythm isn’t just about getting us—it’s about lifting others, too.
